# Chapter 1: The Action Roll

When a hero faces uncertainty, risk, or dramatic tension, you make an **Action Roll** to determine what happens.

This chapter covers everything about the Action Roll—the core resolution mechanic of **Archon**. Everything else in the game builds on this foundation.

**What you'll learn:**
- What an Action Roll is and when to make one
- How to build your dice pool
- The four outcomes (Triumph, Success, Conflict, Setback)
- Boons and Banes (advantage and disadvantage)
- Edge and Expertise (tactical modifiers)
- Probability and what the numbers mean

---

## What Is an Action Roll?

An **Action Roll** resolves a single risky, uncertain, or dramatically significant action.

When you make an Action Roll:
1. You declare what you're doing and how (Action + Approach)
2. The GM assesses your positioning and tells you what you're facing (Vantage)
3. You build a dice pool and roll
4. Everyone interprets the outcome
5. The GM narrates what happens

**This chapter focuses on steps 3-4: building your pool and interpreting results.** See Chapter 2 for Actions and Approaches, and Vantage assessment.

---

## When to Make an Action Roll

Make an Action Roll when a character faces a situation that is **uncertain, risky, or dramatically significant**.

**Make an Action Roll when:**
- The outcome is uncertain
- Failure would create interesting consequences or drama
- Success matters to the story

**Don't make an Action Roll when:**
- The outcome is certain (either assured success or assured failure)
- Failure would just stall progress without adding drama
- The character lacks fundamental capability to attempt the action

These principles are built into **Vantage assessment** (see Chapter 2). When the GM assesses your Vantage as **Certain**, no Action Roll is needed—the outcome is clear. When your Vantage is **Impossible**, you can't attempt this action with this approach.

**Example (no Action Roll needed):**
- A Champion with weapon Expertise attacking a helpless, tied-up prisoner → **Certain** (no Action Roll, automatic success)
- Breaking down a door with unlimited time and no consequences → **Certain** (no Action Roll, eventually succeeds)

**Example (Action Roll required):**
- Same Champion attacking an alert, armored knight → **Uncertain outcome** (Action Roll required)
- Breaking down a door while guards approach → **Time pressure creates drama** (Action Roll required)

## Building Your Dice Pool

When you make an Action Roll, you build a **dice pool** of six-sided dice (d6).

**Your dice pool = Approach + Boons - Banes**

### Starting Dice: Your Approach

Your **Approach** determines how many dice you start with. Approaches are rated 0-5:

- **Approach 3:** Start with 3 dice (skilled)
- **Approach 2:** Start with 2 dice (capable)
- **Approach 1:** Start with 1 die (novice)
- **Approach 0:** Start with 0 dice (untrained)

**See Chapter 2 for complete details on Approaches** (Daring, Grace, Charm, Cunning, Spirit).

---

### Boons (+Dice)

**Boons** represent advantages. Each Boon adds +1 die to your pool.

**Common Boon sources:**
- **Vantage:** Strong Vantage grants 1 Boon (see Chapter 2)
- **Assist:** An ally marks 1 Stress to grant you 1 Boon (see Chapter 4)
- **Push Yourself:** Mark 1 Stress to gain 1 Boon (see Chapter 4)
- **Create an Opening:** Spend 1 Momentum for 1 Boon + 1 Edge (see Chapter 5)
- **Talents:** Some Talents grant Boons in specific situations (see Chapter 9)
- **Favorable circumstances:** GM discretion (ideal tools, perfect positioning, surprise)

**Maximum: +2 Boons**

Multiple Boon sources stack, but you can gain **at most +2 dice from Boons** after all sources are combined.

**Example:**
- Strong Vantage (+1 Boon)
- Ally Assists (+1 Boon)
- You Push Yourself (+1 Boon)
- **Total: +2 Boons** (third Boon is wasted due to cap)

---

### Banes (-Dice)

**Banes** represent disadvantages. Each Bane removes -1 die from your pool.

**Common Bane sources:**
- **Vantage:** Limited Vantage imposes 1-2 Banes (see Chapter 2)
- **Conditions:** Each Condition applies 1 Bane to relevant actions (see Chapter 4)
- **Unfavorable circumstances:** GM discretion (poor tools, bad positioning, hostile environment)

**Maximum: -2 Banes**

Multiple Bane sources stack, but you can lose **at most -2 dice from Banes** after all sources are combined.

---

### Boons and Banes Cancel

**Boons and Banes cancel each other 1:1 before applying the cap.**

1. Count total Boons
2. Count total Banes
3. Cancel 1:1
4. Apply the ±2 cap to whatever remains

**Example 1:**
- Strong Vantage: +1 Boon
- Wounded Condition: -1 Bane
- **Net: 0 modifier** (cancel out)

**Example 2:**
- Strong Vantage: +1 Boon
- Ally Assists: +1 Boon
- Wounded Condition: -1 Bane
- **Net: +1 Boon** (2 Boons - 1 Bane = 1 Boon remaining)

**Example 3:**
- Limited Vantage: -2 Banes
- You Push Yourself: +1 Boon
- Ally Assists: +1 Boon
- Wounded: -1 Bane
- **Calculation:** 2 Boons - 3 Banes = -1 Bane
- **Net: -1 Bane**

---

### Pool Minimum: 2d Take Lowest

**If your pool would be 0 or less** after applying all modifiers, you don't roll 0 dice. Instead:

**Roll 2 dice and take the LOWEST result (instead of highest).**

This represents a desperate, clumsy attempt when you lack capability or favorable circumstances.

**When this happens:**
- Approach 0 with no modifiers
- Approach 1 with 1 Bane
- Approach 2 with 2+ Banes
- Any situation where Banes exceed your Approach + Boons

**Example:**
- Champion with Charm 0 tries to Talk to a guard
- Standard Vantage (no modifier)
- Pool: 0 dice → Roll 2d6, take lowest
- Rolls: 4, 6 → Takes **4** (Conflict)

**Probabilities when rolling 2d take lowest:**
- Setback (1-3): 75%
- Conflict (4-5): 22%
- Success (6): 3%
- Triumph: 0% (impossible)

This creates strong incentive to seek advantages (Assist, Push Yourself, Create an Opening) without hard-locking characters from attempting actions.

---

## Rolling the Dice

Once you've built your pool, roll all your dice at once.

**Check your highest single die** (unless your pool was 0, in which case check your lowest).

---

## The Four Outcomes

Your highest die determines one of four outcomes:

### Triumph (Two or More 6s)

**Heroic success.**

- You accomplish your intent completely
- **Gain 1 Momentum** (see Chapter 5)
- **Ignore all Complications** (see Chapter 2)

**You need to actually roll two 6s**—Triumph cannot be created through Expertise, Edge, or other modifiers. Those can improve your outcome up to Success, but Triumph requires the raw roll.

**Example:**
- Roll 4 dice: 2, 5, 6, 6
- **Triumph** (two 6s)
- You succeed heroically and gain 1 Momentum

---

### Success (At Least One 6)

**Clean success.**

- You accomplish your intent
- No consequences
- No cost

**Example:**
- Roll 3 dice: 2, 4, 6
- **Success** (one 6, no double 6s)
- You succeed cleanly

---

### Conflict (At Least One 4-5, No 6s)

**Success with a Minor Consequence.**

- You accomplish your intent (same as Success)
- **But** you suffer a Minor Consequence:
  - Mark 1 Stress, OR
  - Gain a Tier 1 Condition, OR
  - Lose position/advantage, OR
  - The situation escalates slightly

The GM makes a **soft Impact Move** (see Chapter 11).

**Example:**
- Roll 2 dice: 3, 5
- **Conflict** (highest is 5)
- You succeed, but mark 1 Stress from the strain

---

### Setback (All Dice Show 1-3)

**Failure with a Major Consequence.**

- You **do not** accomplish your intent
- **And** you suffer a Major Consequence:
  - Mark 2 Stress, OR
  - Gain a Tier 2-3 Condition, OR
  - The enemy marks a Progress track against you, OR
  - You lose something important, OR
  - Danger escalates dramatically

The GM makes a **hard Impact Move** (see Chapter 11).

**Example:**
- Roll 2 dice: 2, 3
- **Setback** (all dice 1-3)
- You fail and mark 2 Stress

---

### Disaster (Rare)

**Catastrophic failure.**

**Disaster** occurs when a **Setback** is reduced even further by **Complications** (see Chapter 2). This is rare.

- Taken Out (removed from the scene), OR
- Permanent Burden, OR
- Catastrophic narrative failure

Disaster is the worst possible outcome and should feel appropriately devastating.

---

## Edge (Ignore Complications)

**Edge** lets you ignore **Complications**—enemy defensive abilities that reduce your outcome tier.

**1 Edge = Ignore 1 Complication**

**How to gain Edge:**
- **Create an Opening:** Spend 1 Momentum for 1 Boon + **1 Edge** (see Chapter 5)
- **Some Talents:** Grant Edge in specific circumstances

**How Edge works:**
1. Roll your dice, check outcome
2. GM applies Complications (reduce outcome by 1 tier per Complication)
3. Spend Edge to ignore Complications before they apply
4. Determine final outcome

**Example:**
- You roll Success (rolled a 6)
- Enemy has **Resilient** (1 Complication: tough armor)
- Without Edge: Success → Conflict (reduced by armor)
- **With 1 Edge:** Ignore the Complication, outcome stays **Success**

**Edge is different from Boons:**
- **Boons** give you more dice (better chance of rolling well)
- **Edge** protects your outcome after you roll (negates enemy defenses)

**See Chapter 2 for complete Complication rules.**

---

## Expertise (Convert Setback → Conflict)

**Expertise** represents mastery in a specific domain. When you have Expertise in what you're doing, you succeed more often—but difficult situations still extract a cost.

**Expertise effect:**

**When you roll Setback while acting within your domain of Expertise, convert it to Conflict.**

- You **succeed** at your intent (mark tracks, ask questions, overcome obstacles)
- But you **suffer a Minor Consequence** (mark 1 Stress, lose position, etc.)

**You don't fail, but you pay a price.**

**Example:**
- Champion with **Expertise: Heavy Blades** fights an armored knight
- Rolls: 2, 3 → **Setback** (all dice 1-3)
- **Expertise converts it to Conflict**
- The attack lands (mark Harm track), but you mark 1 Stress from the strain

**Common Expertise domains:**
- Weapons (Heavy Blades, Light Blades, Bows, etc.)
- Skills (Stealth, Tracking, Persuasion, etc.)
- Magic Spheres (for Invokers—see Chapter 10)

**Expertise is granted by:**
- Your Calling's Core Talent (see Chapter 9)
- Additional Talents you choose
- Sometimes by Traits (GM discretion)

**What Expertise doesn't do:**
- Doesn't change your Vantage (you can still have Limited Vantage with Expertise)
- Doesn't eliminate consequences (you still suffer Minor Consequence on converted Conflict)
- Doesn't create Triumph (requires rolling double 6s)

**See Chapter 9 for Calling-specific Expertise.**

## The Complete Action Roll Sequence

Here's how all the pieces fit together when you make an Action Roll:

1. **Declare Action & Approach** (Player)
   - "I attack the knight with my sword, using Daring"

2. **Assess Vantage** (GM)
   - GM determines: Certain / Strong / Standard / Limited / Impossible
   - GM telegraphs consequences

3. **Build Dice Pool** (Player)
   - Start with Approach (e.g., Daring 3 = 3 dice)
   - Add Boons (Strong Vantage = +1, total 4 dice)
   - Remove Banes (Wounded Condition = -1, total 3 dice)
   - Apply Boon/Bane cap (already within ±2)

4. **Roll Dice** (Player)
   - Roll 3d6
   - Check highest die

5. **Determine Raw Outcome** (Everyone)
   - Rolled: 2, 4, 6
   - Highest die is 6 = **Success**

6. **Apply Complications** (GM)
   - Enemy has **Resilient** (1 Complication: tough armor)
   - Success reduced by 1 tier → **Conflict**

7. **Apply Expertise** (Player, if applicable)
   - If you had Expertise and rolled Setback, convert to Conflict
   - (Not applicable in this example—outcome is already Conflict)

8. **Narrate Result** (GM)
   - "Your blade strikes the knight's armor. It connects, but the impact jars your arm. Mark Harm track for the knight, and mark 1 Stress for yourself."

## Probability Tables

Understanding the odds helps you make informed tactical decisions.

### By Dice Pool (No Complications)

| Pool | Setback | Conflict | Success | Triumph |
|------|---------|----------|---------|---------|
| 2d lowest | 75% | 22% | 3% | 0% |
| 1d | 50% | 33% | 17% | 0% |
| 2d | 25% | 37% | 33% | 6% |
| 3d | 12% | 36% | 41% | 11% |
| 4d | 6% | 33% | 44% | 17% |
| 5d | 3% | 28% | 47% | 22% |

**Key Insights:**
- **2d (Standard):** Roughly ⅓ Conflict, ⅓ Success, ⅙ Triumph—balanced odds
- **3d (Standard + 1 Boon):** Setback drops to 12%—competent hero territory
- **4d (Standard + 2 Boons):** Setback at 6%—very reliable
- **0d (2d lowest):** 75% Setback—desperate situation, seek advantages

### Expertise Impact

**With Expertise, Setback converts to Conflict:**

| Pool | Fail Entirely | Conflict | Success | Triumph |
|------|---------------|----------|---------|---------|
| 2d lowest | 0% | 97% | 3% | 0% |
| 1d | 0% | 83% | 17% | 0% |
| 2d | 0% | 62% | 33% | 6% |
| 3d | 0% | 48% | 41% | 11% |
| 4d | 0% | 39% | 44% | 17% |

**Expertise eliminates complete failure**—you always succeed at a cost or better.

## Design Notes: Why Action Rolls Work This Way

**Dice pools feel satisfying.** Rolling a handful of dice is tactile and visual. More dice = clear advantage.

**Highest die is fast.** No math, no adding. Roll, glance, done. Action Rolls resolve quickly.

**Four outcomes create nuance.** Success/failure is binary. The Action Roll gives you four shades of outcome: Triumph/Success/Conflict/Setback.

**Setbacks become rare quickly.** At 3d6, you have 88% chance of Conflict or better. Heroes feel competent without being invincible.

**Triumph requires luck.** You can't guarantee double 6s through modifiers—it always feels special when it happens.

**Edge and Expertise add tactical depth.** The Action Roll rewards preparation. Building advantages matters. Expertise makes mastery feel real.
