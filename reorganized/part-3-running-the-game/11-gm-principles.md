# Chapter 11: GM Principles

> *You're not writing the story alone. You're discovering it with your players.*

This chapter is your foundation as GM — the agendas that guide your decisions, the principles that shape your technique, and the structures that keep sessions flowing. Everything else in Part 3 builds on what's here.

## GM Agendas

Your agendas are your north star. When you're unsure what to do at the table, return to these:

**1. Portray a dangerous, wondrous world.**
Make the world feel alive, reactive, and full of possibility. Danger creates stakes; wonder creates motivation.

**2. Fill the characters' lives with adventure.**
Create drama, meaningful choices, and situations where who these characters are actually matters. Don't wait for players to find the adventure — bring it to them.

**3. Play to find out what happens.**
You are not the author of this story. You set up situations, create pressure, and discover what happens alongside your players. Prep situations, not outcomes.

## GM Principles

These are specific techniques to achieve your agendas. They're not rules — they're habits that make the game better.

### Be a Fan of the Characters

Set up situations where their abilities shine. Make them look cool when they succeed. Don't try to "beat" them — challenge them so their victories feel earned.

This doesn't mean going easy. It means the world is hard *and* the heroes are remarkable. When the Champion faces impossible odds and prevails, that's you being a fan.

### Ask Questions and Build on Answers

"Who taught you to fight?" "What do you fear most?" "Why do you owe the merchant guild?"

Use answers to create NPCs, locations, and plot threads. Collaborative worldbuilding makes players invested because the world contains pieces of them. Every answer is a hook you can pull on later.

### Telegraph Danger, Then Follow Through

Always give players a chance to respond to danger before delivering hard consequences. Use the **Hint → Reveal → Strike** pattern (see **Chapter 12: GM Moves**) and Suspense Moves before Impact Moves.

Exception: When a PC rolls Setback, deliver an immediate consequence — the roll itself was the warning that failed.

### Make Every Roll Matter

Only call for rolls when the outcome is uncertain and both success and failure would be interesting. If failure would just stall progress, don't roll — let them succeed or reframe the situation.

"What do you do?" should always have meaningful stakes attached.

### Embrace the Fiction

Stats and mechanics serve the fiction, not the other way around. If something doesn't make fictional sense, it can't happen — a pixie can't wield a greatsword unless magic explains it, and you can't intimidate a mindless golem.

When in doubt, ask: "Does this make sense in the world?"

### Think Off-Screen Too

The world moves even when PCs aren't watching. Villains advance plans, factions clash, disasters unfold. Create a living world, not a static video game map that only activates when players enter the room.

## When to Roll

The most common mistake new GMs make is calling for too many rolls. The dice come out when an action is **difficult, dangerous, or dramatic** — not for routine tasks.

### Call for a Roll When:

- The outcome is uncertain
- Both success and failure are interesting
- Risk or danger is present
- The moment is dramatically important

### Say Yes When:

- Success is trivial for this character
- Failure would just stall progress
- There's no time pressure or danger
- The action is routine

### Say No When:

- The action makes no fictional sense
- It's impossible given established facts
- It would break the core fiction or tone

**Examples:**

- "I climb the ladder" → Say yes (unless it's trapped, rickety, or under fire)
- "I jump across the 30-foot chasm" → Call for a roll (dangerous, uncertain)
- "I flap my arms and fly" → Say no (the fiction doesn't support it)

## Failure Is Not a Wall

When a PC fails, the **situation changes**. Never leave them in the same spot with no progress.

**Bad failure:**
- "You fail to pick the lock." (stall — nothing happens, player is stuck)
- "The guard doesn't believe you." (no consequence, no new situation)

**Good failure:**
- "You trigger the trap. Mark 2 Stress and the alarm rings." (consequence + new situation)
- "The guard believes you're lying and calls for backup. What do you do?" (escalation + new choice)

Failure should always push the fiction forward. The situation worsens, new problems emerge, or the approach must change — but the game never grinds to a halt.

## One Roll Per Dramatic Question

One of the most common mistakes in fiction-first games: breaking a single dramatic moment into multiple micro-rolls that drain tension and create attrition spirals.

### The Principle

**One roll resolves one complete dramatic question or objective.**

- "Cross the collapsing hallway" is **one roll**, not separate rolls for dodging each falling timber
- "Navigate the treacherous canyon pass" is **one roll**, not one per obstacle
- "Escape the burning building" is **one roll**, not one per room
- "Sneak past the guard patrol" is **one roll**, not one per guard

Once a character rolls to handle a specific threat environment, don't force additional rolls for micro-elements within that same threat.

### Why This Matters

Multiple micro-rolls create attrition spirals. Five rolls to cross a dangerous zone means five chances to fail. Each failure marks Stress or imposes Conditions. By the end, PCs are battered before they reach the real encounter. Tension drains into tedium.

One dramatic roll creates stakes. The outcome determines the cost in a single moment:

- **Triumph:** Heroic crossing, gain advantage
- **Success:** Clean success, no complication
- **Conflict:** You make it across, but mark 1 Stress
- **Setback:** You make it, but take 2 Stress + a Condition

### When to Call for Multiple Rolls

**Multiple rolls are right when:**
- Each roll addresses a **distinct dramatic question** (escape the mine → fight the orcs → convince the guards = three separate problems)
- A **multi-stage challenge** has truly separate obstacles (break the wards, fight the cultists, destroy the altar)
- **Different PCs tackle different parts** (Rogue picks lock, Fighter keeps watch, Sage dispels alarm)

**One roll is right when:**
- There's a **single dramatic objective** (crossing a dangerous zone, escaping pursuit)
- Micro-elements are part of the **same threat** (each falling beam, each crack in ice, each guard in patrol)
- Multiple rolls would only create **attrition** without meaningful choice

## Assessing Vantage

When a player declares an action, assess their external fictional positioning before they roll. Vantage reflects tools, circumstances, and tactical situation — not character skill (that's Approaches).

- **Certain** → Auto-success, no roll needed
- **Strong** → Roll with 1 Boon
- **Standard** → Standard roll, no modifier
- **Limited** → Roll with 1-2 Banes
- **Impossible** → Cannot attempt with this approach

**Three Quick Questions:**

1. **Can they attempt this?** (Do they have capability, tools, positioning?) If no → Impossible.
2. **What's their Vantage?** (Given who they are and the situation.) Assign Boons/Banes.
3. **Is failure interesting?** (Would failure create drama?) If no → Don't roll, let it happen.

**Examples:**

- "I pick the lock" without tools → Limited (1 Bane)
- "I swing sword" with broken arm → Impossible → "That won't work, try something else"
- Convincing a guard who doesn't speak your language → Impossible via Talk → Suggest a different approach

For the complete Vantage framework with detailed examples and edge cases, see **Part 4: Vantage Reference**.

## Session Structure

### Opening (5 minutes)

- **Recap** the previous session (2-3 minutes, invite player contributions)
- **Frame the first scene** — in media res or natural follow-up from last session's cliffhanger
- Ask "What do you do?"

### Middle (Bulk of session)

- Alternate between **action/tension** and **breathing room**
- Use Suspense Moves to build tension (see **Chapter 12**)
- Use Impact Moves to deliver consequences
- Ask clarifying questions, build on answers
- Track pacing: if the table energy drops, escalate or shift scenes

### Closing (10 minutes)

- Find a natural breaking point — not mid-combat
- Preview next session if appropriate ("Next time: the vault awaits...")
- **End of Session questions** (optional, for advancement):
  - "Did we learn something important about the world?"
  - "Did we overcome a significant threat?"
  - "Did a character relationship change meaningfully?"

## Common GM Pitfalls

**Rolling for everything.** Not every action needs a roll. Save dice for meaningful moments. If there's no risk and no drama, just say what happens.

**Skipping telegraphs.** Always use a Suspense Move before an Impact Move, unless the danger was already clearly established. Players should see consequences coming.

**Arbitrary consequences.** Hard moves should follow from the fiction, not GM fiat. If the consequence doesn't make sense given what's happening in the story, it feels unfair.

**Stalling on failure.** "You fail" is not a move. Every failed roll must change the situation — new problems, escalation, or forced change of approach.

**Prescribing actions.** Don't say "Roll Defy." Present the situation, ask "What do you do?", then determine the appropriate Action from their response.

**Railroading outcomes.** Players make choices, you respond. If you've predetermined the outcome, why are they rolling? Prep situations, not conclusions.

**Every fight is epic.** Minions should go down fast. Reserve complex encounters for bosses and set-pieces. If every combat takes an hour, none of them feel special.

**Slow bookkeeping.** Trust players to track their own resources. Audit occasionally, don't micromanage every Stress box.
