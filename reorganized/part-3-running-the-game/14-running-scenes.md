# Chapter 14: Running Scenes

> *The dice tell you the cost, not whether the story moves forward.*

Different scene types demand different GM techniques. A murder investigation plays nothing like a tense negotiation, which plays nothing like a desperate chase. This chapter gives you frameworks for the most common scene types so you can run them with confidence.

## Investigation Scenes

Investigation is uniquely vulnerable to bad rulings. A GM who gates plot progress on a successful Know or Sense roll creates exactly the "failure stalls progress" problem the game warns against. This framework prevents that.

### Core Principle: Always Give Them Something

**Never gate essential clues behind rolls.** If the PCs are in the right place and looking for information, they find it. Rolls determine:

- **How complete** the information is (Setback = partial, Success = full picture)
- **How fast** they get it (Conflict = time pressure, Success = immediate)
- **What it costs** (Conflict/Setback = complications, attract attention, trigger danger)

### The Investigation Framework

**1. They're in the right place → Give the clue automatically.**

"You search the study and find the coded letter in the desk drawer." No roll needed — they're searching the right location.

**2. They need to interpret or connect clues → Call for Know.**

- Success: Full understanding + ask 1 clarifying question
- Conflict: Partial understanding, time pressure or complication
- Setback: Misinterpretation AND trigger danger (alarm, witness, trap)

**3. They need to notice something hidden or subtle → Call for Sense.**

- Success: Notice hidden detail + ask 2 questions
- Conflict: Notice it but trigger complication (someone sees you, time passes)
- Setback: Miss the detail AND complication (false lead, attract attention)

### The Three-Clue Rule

For any conclusion you want PCs to reach, plant at least **three independent clues** that point to it. If they miss one or two, they still have paths forward.

**Example — "Who killed the merchant?"**

- Clue 1: Coded letter in victim's study (Know to decode)
- Clue 2: Witness saw cloaked figure (Talk to get description)
- Clue 3: Poison residue on cup (Sense to detect, Know to identify)

All three point to the same suspect. Missing one doesn't stall the investigation.

### Failed Rolls Don't Stop Progress

Failed rolls **escalate** — they never stall.

- Failed to decode the letter? → The letter gets stolen, but the thief's identity is a new clue.
- Failed to notice the poison? → The poisoner strikes again, revealing their method.
- Failed to get witness cooperation? → The witness is murdered, but the killer made a mistake.

Every "failure" changes the situation and opens new paths — the stakes get higher, but progress continues.

### Investigation Principles

**1. Information flows forward, complications flow sideways.** Rolls never stop information — they add costs or redirect paths.

**2. Failed rolls escalate, don't stall.** Missing a clue makes things more dangerous, not impossible.

**3. Let players be smart.** If they deduce the answer from available clues, don't demand rolls to "confirm" it.

**4. Mysteries have momentum.** Time pressure keeps investigation moving (the ritual completes in 3 days, the assassin strikes tonight).

**5. Know and Sense are your investigation tools.** Know = past facts, lore, off-screen information. Sense = present observation, hidden details, connecting evidence.

### Common Investigation Actions

**Searching a Location:** Don't roll for obvious clues in the right room. Roll Sense only if the clue is genuinely hidden. Setback = trigger trap, alert guards, or waste time.

**Interrogating a Witness:** Talk (persuasion) or Clash (intimidation) against their Will track. Conflict = partial info but they demand something. Setback = they clam up AND alert conspirators.

**Decoding Evidence:** Know to interpret clues. Conflict = partial understanding, need additional resources. Setback = misinterpret (false lead) AND complication.

**Following a Lead:** Sense to track or tail. Conflict = find them but they know you're coming. Setback = lose the trail AND complication (ambush, wrong location).

## Social Conflict

Social conflicts use Will tracks (see **Chapter 13**). When a Will track fills, the adversary's resistance collapses — they flee, surrender, concede, or break down.

### What Social Conflict Can Achieve

Filling a Will track represents overcoming resistance in this specific moment:

- **Concession** — They accept your position in this matter
- **Cooperation** — They work with you on a specific goal
- **Surrender** — They yield to your demands right now
- **Withdrawal** — They disengage rather than continue
- **Temporary Agreement** — They go along for now

**What it cannot achieve:**
- Core belief changes ("The priest now worships your god")
- Permanent loyalty shifts ("They'll never oppose you again")
- Identity rewrites ("The lifelong enemy is now your friend")

These require magic, Talents, or long-term roleplay investment — multiple scenes, favors granted, trust built over time.

### Talk vs Clash for Will

Both mark Will, but the outcome and relationship implications differ:

**Talk** (persuasion, negotiation, influence): When successful, they *agree* or become *allied*. Positive relationship possible.

**Clash** (intimidation, taunts, psychological pressure): When successful, they're *broken*, *flee*, or *surrender*. Relationship damaged, coerced.

**Switching mid-track:** Switching from Talk to Clash (or vice versa) doesn't reset progress — you're chipping away at resolve regardless of method. If the switch creates obvious contradiction (building trust → suddenly threatening), impose 1 Bane on the new approach.

### Will-Based Complications

These represent psychological defenses:

- **Resolute** — Disciplined, stubborn, faithful. Applies to persuasion, intimidation, deception, magical influence.
- **Vigilant** — Well-informed, suspicious. Applies to deception, lies, hidden motives.

Handle like physical Complications — Create an Opening, exploit weaknesses, or use teamwork.

### Telegraphing Social Outcomes

Let players know what they're working toward: "The captain is sweating now, his confidence clearly shaken — one more push and he'll break." This keeps social conflicts engaging and gives players a sense of progress.

## Battlegrounds

Instead of drawing grids, define combat areas with **Features** and **Threats**. This creates rich tactical combat without minis or maps.

### Features (Tactical Elements)

Terrain or objects PCs can use:

- "Crumbling pillars" — cover, can be toppled onto enemies
- "Chandelier" — can be cut down, dramatic swing
- "Narrow bridge" — limits movement, risk of falling
- "High ground" — grants Boon when attacking from above

### Threats (Environmental Hazards)

Dangers that can be triggered as Suspense or Impact Moves:

- "Lava pits" — fall in = mark 3 Stress + Burned
- "Unstable floor" — collapses under too many combatants
- "Swinging blades" — triggers on Setback in this zone

### Example Battleground: The Burning Library

**Features:**
- Bookshelves (cover, can push onto enemies)
- Balcony (high ground, grants Boon)
- Spiral staircase (choke point, limits flanking)

**Threats:**
- Spreading fire (marks 1 Stress per round in burning zones)
- Smoke (Environmental Complication: Elusive, hinders Sense/ranged)
- Collapsing ceiling (Impact Move: 2 Stress, zone becomes impassable)

**How it plays:** On Conflict results, fire spreads to a new zone. On Setback, trigger a Threat (falling beam, smoke inhalation, floor collapse). Features give players tactical options — push an enemy into fire, take cover behind shelves, fight from the balcony.

### Defining Battlegrounds

When combat begins in a significant location, quickly establish:

1. **2-3 Features** — What can the PCs *use*?
2. **1-2 Threats** — What might *hurt* them?
3. **Zones** (if using them) — Where are the distinct areas?

You don't need all of this for every fight. A roadside ambush might just have "tree cover" and "the cliff edge." Save detailed battlegrounds for set-piece encounters.

## Progress and Countdown Tracks

Beyond Challenge tracks, you'll use Progress and Countdown tracks to structure extended situations.

### Progress Tracks

PCs fill these through successful actions. They measure advancement toward a goal.

**Examples:**
- "Navigate the labyrinth" (6 ticks)
- "Earn the merchant's trust" (4 ticks)
- "Repair the airship" (8 ticks)
- "Decipher the ancient map" (4 ticks)

**Mark ticks when:** PCs succeed at relevant actions (typically on Success or Conflict). Greater Effect marks 2 instead of 1.

### Countdown Tracks

GM-facing timers. Something happens when they fill — usually bad.

**Examples:**
- "The ritual completes" (8 ticks)
- "Reinforcements arrive" (4 ticks)
- "The dam breaks" (6 ticks)
- "Dawn breaks" (10 ticks)

**Mark ticks when:** Time passes, PCs fail, or the fiction demands it (typically on Conflict/Setback, or each round).

### Track Sizing

Track size determines how long the situation lasts:

- **4 ticks** = Short (2-3 actions, quick tension)
- **6 ticks** = Medium (4-5 actions, sustained engagement)
- **8 ticks** = Long (6-7 actions, extended scene)
- **10+ ticks** = Epic (full-session arc)

### Combining Tracks for Tension

The best scenes run **multiple tracks simultaneously**:

- Progress "Escape the dungeon" (6 ticks) vs. Countdown "Ceiling collapses" (4 ticks)
- Progress "Convince the council" (6 ticks) vs. Countdown "Assassin arrives" (8 ticks)
- Progress "Solve the puzzle" (4 ticks) vs. Countdown "Acid fills the room" (6 ticks)

The tension comes from racing one track against the other. Players feel the pressure of the Countdown while working toward the Progress goal.

## Scene Framing

### Starting Scenes

Open with context, then hand control to players:

1. **Establish the situation** — Where are they? What's happening? What's at stake?
2. **Present a prompt** — Something that demands response (danger, opportunity, arrival)
3. **Ask "What do you do?"** — Then wait.

**Start in media res when possible.** "You burst through the door into the throne room. The king lies bleeding on the floor. The assassin is halfway out the window. What do you do?" is better than "You approach the castle gates. They're open."

### Ending Scenes

Scenes end when:
- The dramatic question is resolved (track filled, objective achieved or failed)
- The situation changes completely (new location, new threat, time skip)
- Energy drops and the moment is spent

Cut away at high points when you can. "The door slams open and you see — we'll pick up there next time" keeps momentum between sessions.

### Pacing

**Alternate tension and relief.** After an intense combat, give players a moment to breathe — Short Rest, roleplay, planning. After quiet exploration, introduce pressure — a sound, a discovery, a Countdown starting.

**If energy drops at the table:** Escalate. Introduce a new threat, advance a Countdown, or cut to a new scene. Don't let momentum die waiting for players to figure out what to do next.

**If the table feels overwhelmed:** De-escalate. Offer a moment of safety, a clear next step, or an NPC who helps.
