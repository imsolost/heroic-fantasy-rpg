# Stakes: Framing Danger

**Stakes frame the danger of a scene, telling the table about the severity of trouble they should expect.**

This can be implied through storytelling cues as you set the scene, or stated explicitly using the categories below. The greater the stakes, the clearer this communication should be.

**Unless stated otherwise, the scene has Regular Stakes.**

---

## The Three Stakes Levels

**Note on Narrative Consequences:** A narrative consequence means something goes wrong in the fiction without marking Stress or inflicting Conditions. Examples: takes longer, makes noise, attracts attention, looks foolish, wastes resources, loses positioning.

### Low Stakes
**Lighter territory. Reduced consequences.**

**Consequence Tier:** Reduce by 1 step (minimum: narrative consequence)
- **Conflict** → Minor - 1 = **Narrative consequence only** (no Stress)
- **Setback** → Major - 1 = **Minor Consequence** (1 Stress, lose position, complication)
- **Disaster** → Severe - 1 = **Major Consequence** (2 Stress, Condition, serious setback)

**Examples:**
- A bar fight breaks out
- Questioned by a friendly guard
- Climbing with a safety rope
- Haggling with a merchant
- Training spar

---

### Regular Stakes
**Typical play. The assumed default.**

**Consequence Tier:** Use standard Consequence framework (no adjustment)
- **Conflict** → **Minor Consequence** (1 Stress, lose position, complication)
- **Setback** → **Major Consequence** (2 Stress, Condition, serious setback)
- **Disaster** → **Severe Consequence** (Taken Out, Burden, catastrophic failure)

**Examples:**
- Sneaking through a guarded warehouse
- Tense negotiation with hostile faction
- Combat against bandits or trained guards
- Climbing a cliff face
- Disarming a trap

---

### High Stakes
**Things are escalated. Serious danger.**

**Consequence Tier:** Increase by 1 step (maximum: Severe)
- **Conflict** → Minor + 1 = **Major Consequence** (2 Stress, Condition, serious setback)
- **Setback** → Major + 1 = **Severe Consequence** (Taken Out, Burden, catastrophic failure)
- **Disaster** → Severe + 1 = **Severe Consequence** (cannot exceed Severe—often Taken Out + Burden)

**Examples:**
- Cornered by an angry ogre
- Explaining your actions to the queen (failure = execution)
- Boss fight against ancient dragon
- Leaping across a lava chasm with no safety net
- Holding the gate alone against an army

---

## Consequence Tier Boundaries

**Consequence Tiers cannot be reduced below Minor or increased above Severe.**

- **Below Minor** → Becomes narrative consequence (no mechanical penalty)
- **Above Severe** → Remains Severe (though GM can make Severe consequences more devastating narratively)

**Examples:**
- Low Stakes Conflict: Minor - 1 = narrative only (no Stress marked)
- High Stakes Disaster: Severe + 1 = still Severe (but GM might rule Taken Out + Burden instead of just Taken Out)

---

## How to Use Stakes

### Communicate Stakes Through Fiction

**Most of the time, you don't need to explicitly say "This is High Stakes."** Let the fiction communicate the danger:

- "The dragon's eyes glow with ancient fury as it rears back—its breath could turn you to ash"
- "The queen's hand hovers near the executioner's bell. One wrong word..."
- "The lava chasm yawns below—30 feet across, no rope, no second chances"

The description makes the stakes clear.

### When to State Stakes Explicitly

**For High Stakes moments, say it out loud:**

- "This is High Stakes—if you fail, you're Taken Out."
- "The lava chasm is High Stakes. Success = you make it. Conflict = you make it but mark 2 Stress. Setback = you fall—Taken Out."

**For Low Stakes moments, signal the lighter tone:**

- "This is Low Stakes—just a training exercise. Conflict won't mark Stress."
- "The guard is friendly and half-asleep. Low Stakes—worst case, he gets suspicious."

**Regular Stakes is the default—no need to state it.**

---

## When to Adjust Stakes

### Use Low Stakes When:
- **Training or practice** (sparring, climbing with safety rope)
- **Routine challenges** (friendly social interactions, minor obstacles)
- **Pacing relief** (after intense sequence, give breathing room)
- **Teaching moments** (first encounter with new mechanic)
- **PCs already struggling** (multiple wounded, low Stress—don't grind them down further)

### Use High Stakes When:
- **Boss fights or elite enemies** (ancient dragon, legendary warrior)
- **Life-threatening hazards** (lava chasms, deadly traps with no safety net)
- **Climactic confrontations** (BBEG at ritual climax, explaining yourself to the queen)
- **Last-ditch efforts** (holding the gate alone, one shot to stop the bomb)
- **Fiction demands it** (genuinely life-or-death moment)

### Default to Regular Stakes

Most rolls use Regular Stakes. Only adjust when fiction clearly demands it.

**Don't escalate arbitrarily.** High Stakes should feel rare and earned.

**Don't de-escalate to avoid consequences.** If the situation is dangerous, use Regular or High Stakes.

---

## Stakes and Complications

**Stakes and Complications are separate systems.**

- **Stakes** determine consequence severity (Low/Regular/High)
- **Complications** determine how often you face consequences (0, 1, 2, 3+ Complications)

**High Stakes + High Complications = Extremely Dangerous**
- Elite boss with 2 Complications and High Stakes
- Even Success outcomes can become Disaster
- Signal to players: "This is incredibly dangerous—use teamwork, Momentum, alternate approaches"

**Low Stakes + Low Complications = Routine Challenge**
- Minor obstacle with 0 Complications and Low Stakes
- Conflict = minor setback, Setback = small cost
- Players can afford to take risks

## Examples in Play

### Example 1: Climbing

**Low Stakes (Training Wall with Safety Rope):**
- Conflict: Take longer, look clumsy (narrative consequence)
- Setback: Slip and catch yourself, mark 1 Stress from embarrassment

**Regular Stakes (Cliff Face):**
- Conflict: Slip but catch yourself, mark 1 Stress
- Setback: Fall partway, mark 2 Stress

**High Stakes (Cliff Over Lava Pit):**
- Conflict: Slip and barely catch yourself, mark 2 Stress from heat and strain
- Setback: Fall into lava, Taken Out

### Example 2: Social Conflict

**Low Stakes (Friendly Merchant):**
- Conflict: Bad deal but they're still friendly (narrative consequence)
- Setback: Offend them slightly, mark 1 Stress or they refuse further business

**Regular Stakes (Hostile Noble):**
- Conflict: Lose credibility, mark 1 Stress
- Setback: They call guards, mark 2 Stress

**High Stakes (Enemy Warlord):**
- Conflict: They order your arrest, mark 2 Stress
- Setback: They execute you on the spot, Taken Out

### Example 3: Combat

**Low Stakes (Training Spar):**
- Conflict: Embarrassed, look foolish (narrative consequence)
- Setback: Bruised ego or body, mark 1 Stress

**Regular Stakes (Bandit Ambush):**
- Conflict: Mark 1 Stress, enemy presses advantage
- Setback: Mark 2 Stress, enemy disarms you

**High Stakes (Boss Fight with Ancient Demon):**
- Conflict: Mark 2 Stress, demon's power escalates
- Setback: Taken Out, demon prepares killing blow

## Consequence Variety

**Stakes adjusts severity, not variety.** Use different consequence types at every stakes level:

**Low Stakes Consequences:**
- Narrative setbacks (take longer, make noise, lose tool, look foolish)
- Social complications (embarrassment, minor offense)
- Resource costs (consume item, time passes)
- **1 Stress** (only on Setback, and only if it makes sense)

**Regular Stakes Consequences:**
- Mark Stress (1 on Conflict, 2 on Setback)
- Inflict Conditions (tier 1-2)
- Lose position (separated, surrounded, disadvantaged)
- Complicate objectives (alarm raised, countdown advances)

**High Stakes Consequences:**
- Heavy Stress (2 on Conflict, Taken Out on Setback)
- Burdens (tier 2-3 persistent Conditions)
- Catastrophic failure (objective fails completely, ally endangered, ritual completes)
- Permanent loss (in extreme cases with narrative justification)

**Remember:** Marking Stress is the fallback when you can't think of something more interesting—not the primary way to drive drama.

---

## Summary

**Stakes frame the danger of a scene.**

- **Low Stakes:** Reduced consequence severity, Conflict = narrative only
- **Regular Stakes:** Standard consequences (default, no adjustment)
- **High Stakes:** Increased consequence severity, Conflict = 2 Stress

**Key Principle:** "The greater the stakes, the clearer the communication should be."

**Golden Rule:** Stakes reflect fictional danger. Let the fiction communicate stakes most of the time. State it explicitly for High Stakes moments.
